Marilyn Monroe was born Norma Jeane Mortenson on June 1, 1926, at the Los Angeles General Hospital in Los Angeles, California. Her mother, Gladys Pearl Baker (née Monroe; 1902–1984), was born in Piedras Negras, Coahuila, Mexico, to a poor Midwestern family who migrated to California at the turn of the century.At age 15, Gladys had married John Newton Baker, an abusive man for nine years her senior. They had two children together, Robert (1918–1933) and Berniece (1919–2014). She successfully filed for divorce and sole custody of her two oldest in 1923, but Baker kidnapped the children soon after and moved with them to Kentucky.

Her TeenAge years

Monroe spent her childhood in various orphanages and foster homes,Moving in with family friend Grace McKee Goddard at age 11 changed her fate. “It wasn’t till later that I realized how much she had done for me,” Monroe wrote of her “Aunt Grace” in her posthumously published memoir, My Story. “If not for Grace I would have been sent to a state or country institution where there are fewer privileges, such as being allowed to have a Christmas tree or seeing a movie sometimes. I lived in the orphanage only off and on. Most of the time I was placed with a family, who were given five dollars a week for keeping me. I was placed in nine different families before I was able to quit being a legal orphan. I did this at sixteen by getting married.” (Monroe’s only guardian in the film is a neighbor who appears briefly, played by Sara Paxton.) When McKee Goddard and her husband announced their move to West Virginia they offered a then 15-year-old Monroe the choice between marrying James Dougherty, the 21-year-old son of a former neighbor, or returning to the orphanage. She chose marriage.They wed in 1942 and split by 1946.

How did she get her name?

When selecting a stage name, Norma Jeane decided on Monroe, her mother’s maiden name. However, this choice was just one aspect of her past that she revealed—initially, at least. Reflecting on this period in her memoir, My Story, Monroe admitted to fabricating details in her interviews, particularly regarding her parents. "I used to tell lies in my interviews—mostly about my mother and father," she wrote. "I’d claim she was deceased—and he was somewhere in Europe. I lied because I felt ashamed to reveal that my mother was institutionalized—and that I was born 'out of wedlock' and never had any contact with my estranged father."
