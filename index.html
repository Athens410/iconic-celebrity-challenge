Marilyn Monroe was born Norma Jeane Mortenson on June 1, 1926, at the Los Angeles General Hospital in Los Angeles, California. Her mother, Gladys Pearl Baker (née Monroe; 1902–1984), was born in Piedras Negras, Coahuila, Mexico, to a poor Midwestern family who migrated to California at the turn of the century. At age 15, Gladys had married John Newton Baker, an abusive man for nine years her senior. They had two children together, Robert (1918–1933) and Berniece (1919–2014). She successfully filed for divorce and sole custody of her two oldest in 1923, but Baker kidnapped the children soon after and moved with them to Kentucky.

Her Childhood

Throughout her upbringing, Marilyn endured a series of foster placements and experienced neglect and abuse. Despite these challenges, she found solace in her love for reading and cinema, often escaping into the fantasy world of Hollywood films. This early exposure to the world of entertainment sparked her desire to become an actress from a young age.


Her Teenage years

At the age of 16, Marilyn married James Dougherty, a neighbor's son, in an attempt to find stability and to try and start a different life,away from the orphanage. However, the marriage ended in divorce several years later. During her teenage years, Marilyn worked various jobs, including as a factory worker during World War II. It was during this time that she was discovered by a photographer, which led to her initial foray into modeling.

How did she get her name?

When selecting a stage name, Norma Jeane decided on Monroe, her mother’s maiden name. However, this choice was just one aspect of her past that she revealed—initially, at least. Reflecting on this period in her memoir, My Story, Monroe admitted to fabricating details in her interviews, particularly regarding her parents. "I used to tell lies in my interviews—mostly about my mother and father," she wrote. "I’d claim she was deceased—and he was somewhere in Europe. I lied because I felt ashamed to reveal that my mother was institutionalized—and that I was born 'out of wedlock' and never had any contact with my estranged father."

Marilyn Monroe began her early career as a model. She signed with Blue Book Modeling Agency in 1946, where she appeared in various advertisements and magazine covers.

By 1950, Marilyn Monroe was on the cusp of becoming a household name. Her strategic career choices and persistence began to bear fruit. She landed more substantial roles in films such as "All About Eve" and "The Asphalt Jungle," where her performances received critical acclaim and showcased her talent to both the public and industry insiders. These roles significantly enhanced her profile and proved her acting abilities, marking her transition from a model and minor actress to a rising star in Hollywood. The year 1950 solidified Marilyn's foothold in the entertainment industry, setting the stage for her ascent to icon status in the years that followed. Each step and decision, from modeling to acting in minor roles, and then breaking through with more significant parts, was a calculated move towards achieving stardom.




Marilyn Monroe's star burned brightly, illuminating the realms of film, fashion, and popular culture with her undeniable talent and captivating presence!

During this time, Marilyn solidified her status as a Hollywood icon through a series of standout performances in films such as "As Young as You Feel," "Niagra," "Clash by Night," "Gentlemen Prefer Blondes," "How to Marry a Millionaire," "River of No Return," "There's No Business Like Show Business," and "The Seven Year Itch." Her roles showcased her versatility as an actress, from comedic and dramatic performances to musical numbers that left audiences spellbound.


In 1962, the world was shaken by the untimely passing of Marilyn Monroe, marking the end of an era. Despite the challenges she faced in her personal life, Monroe continued to captivate audiences with her unparalleled talent and beauty. In the final year of her life, she embarked on new projects, including her last completed film, "Something's Got to Give," which showcased her comedic prowess and emotional depth. However, her tragic death at the age of 36 left a void in the hearts of millions, reminding us of the fragility of life and the enduring legacy she left behind.Her legacy as an icon of talent, Intelligence, beauty, grace, and resilience endures, serving as a source of inspiration for generations to come. While her time on this earth may have been brief, her spirit lives on through her timeless films, iconic photographs, and enduring influence on popular culture. As we reflect on her life from 1926 to 1962, we are reminded of the power of authenticity, compassion, and self-expression.
